Become an Essentialist

Introduction

Welcome to this journey of Essentialism! This course is designed to help you simplify your life by focusing on what truly matters.

Inspired by the insights of Greg McKeown and others, you'll go through exercises, reflections, and questions that help you identify your 'vital few' from the 'trivial many.'

Each section of this course will focus on a key principle from Essentialism.

Section 1: Identifying the "Vital Few"

The first principle we'll delve into is recognizing that in life, only a handful of things are truly important.

In a world filled with noise, distractions, and endless opportunities, it's easy to spread ourselves too thin. But the secret to a fulfilling life lies in identifying your "vital few" — the essential elements that bring you joy, fulfillment, and meaning.

The following journal prompts aim to guide you in this enlightening journey of discovery.

Becoming An Essentialist: Journal Prompts

1. Identifying Your Sources of Joy and Fulfillment

Understanding what truly makes you happy is the first step toward a life of focused value.

What are the top 3 activities that bring you the most joy and fulfillment?

List these activities, and for each one, write a paragraph explaining why it brings you joy and how it aligns with your values or desires.

2. Time Allocation: Reality Check

Often, there is a mismatch between what we value most and where we actually spend our time.

What do you spend the most time on currently?

Is there alignment with your top 3 activities?

Break down an average day or week into different activities and estimate the time spent on each. Then evaluate whether these align with your top 3 joy-inducing activities. If they don't, write down your thoughts on why that's the case.

3. Reflecting on Value

“The overwhelming reality is: we live in a world where almost everything is worthless and a very few things are exceptionally valuable.”

This quote pushes us to reflect on what is genuinely valuable in our lives amidst a sea of distractions.

Reflect on this quote. What are the 'exceptionally valuable' things in your life?

Write down a list of items, activities, or people you consider exceptionally valuable in your life. For each, add a sentence or two explaining why it holds such high value for you.

4. The 80/20 Principle and You

The 80/20 principle, also known as the Pareto Principle, suggests that 80% of results come from 20% of efforts.

How can the 80/20 principle apply to your personal life?

Identify areas of your life where you think the 80/20 principle might apply. Is it in your work, relationships, or personal growth perhaps? Write down how focusing on the 'vital few' efforts could dramatically improve your results in these areas.

5. Eliminating the Noise

“Before you can zero in on your 'vital few,' it's important to identify and eliminate the 'trivial many'.”

What are the things you consider ‘noise’ that need to be eliminated to focus on your 'vital few'?

Make a list of activities, commitments, or distractions you consider to be 'noise' in your life. Next to each, write a short action plan on how you can either eliminate or minimize this noise.

Section 2: Do Less, But Do It Better

The next chapter in our journey explores the principle of concentrated effort: "Do Less, But Do It Better." In a culture that often glorifies multitasking and the 'hustle,' there's profound wisdom in refining your focus. Mastering a few things, as opposed to being a jack-of-all-trades, can bring a greater sense of accomplishment and satisfaction.

This section's journal prompts will guide you in re-evaluating your current commitments, identifying your most significant focus, and taking actionable steps to hone your expertise.

1. The Essence of Mastery

This quote pushes us to imagine a world where we can excel but only in a single domain.

Answer this question for yourself.


Take a few moments to reflect on this question deeply. Write down that one thing, and then elaborate on why you chose it. What does achieving mastery in this area mean for you, and how could it transform your life?

2. Know Your Priorities

Life throws many 'important' tasks at us, but they can't all be top priorities.

How many priorities do you have right now? List them.

Create a list of your current priorities in all spheres of your life—personal, professional, relational, etc. Try to order them based on their importance to you. Include a short sentence next to each to clarify why it holds the position it does in your list.

3. The Trap of the "Rabbits"

Chasing too many goals simultaneously often leads to achieving none, much like a dog chasing two rabbits catches neither.

Which of your current priorities can be considered 'rabbits' that you are unsuccessfully chasing?

Review your list of priorities and identify which ones are 'rabbits,' or distractions that you're not making real progress on. Write down these identified 'rabbits' and a sentence or two about why you think each has become a diversion.

4. Delegate or Eliminate

The key to doing less but doing it better often lies in eliminating the unnecessary or delegating what others can do.

What can you delegate or eliminate to focus on your 'one thing'?

Review your list of priorities and 'rabbits' and decide what tasks or commitments can be either delegated to someone else or eliminated altogether. Write an action plan next to each, detailing how you'll go about delegating or eliminating it.

5. Stacking the Odds

Success rarely happens by chance. It's about creating conditions that favor you.

How can you stack the probabilities in your favor to succeed in your most important pursuit?

Think deeply about your 'one thing'—the area where you want to achieve mastery. List down steps, strategies, or conditions that can improve your odds of success in this area. This could involve resources you'll need, mentors you'll seek, habits you'll form, or pitfalls you'll avoid.

Section 3: You'll Never Get It All Done (So Don't Even Try)

The third principle, "You'll Never Get It All Done (So Don't Even Try)," challenges the societal myth of the "superhuman" who can handle every task, opportunity, and challenge flawlessly. Instead, it asks you to embrace the liberating concept of prioritization—of admitting that some things will not get done, and that's perfectly okay. By giving yourself the permission to focus only on what's essential, you pave the way for more meaningful, high-quality contributions to your life and the lives of others.

1. The Liberation of Limitation

This quote highlights the freedom that comes with letting go of the need to be all things to all people.

How does this quote resonate with you?

Reflect on your immediate emotional response to this quote. Do you agree with it? Do you find it liberating, uncomfortable, or challenging? Write down your thoughts, and explain why you feel the way you do.

2. Traps and Distractions

Opportunities can sometimes be distractions in disguise, especially when they don't align with your main goals.

What tasks or opportunities are currently pulling you off track?

List out the activities, commitments, or opportunities that are taking your time and energy but not contributing to your primary objectives. Be honest with yourself—these could be things you enjoy but that are not serving your bigger picture.

3. The Paradox of Success

Sometimes, success can be a double-edged sword, creating more demands on your time and focus.

How has your success made it difficult for you to maintain focus on what's essential?

Reflect on the ways your accomplishments have made your life more complex. Write down examples of how success has brought about distractions or a dilution of your focus, and how you might address this moving forward.

4. The Art of Saying No

The ability to say "no" is a critical skill in maintaining your focus on what's truly important.

What would 'saying no' look like in your life right now?

Imagine scenarios where you could say "no" to opportunities, tasks, or commitments that don't serve your primary focus. Describe what those situations would look like and how saying "no" would make you feel. Also, consider the positive impacts this act might have on your time and energy.

5. The Essentiality Audit

Imagine your life as a garden. Just as you would weed a garden to allow the flowers to bloom, similarly, you need to remove or reduce certain tasks or expectations to let your main goals flourish.

What tasks or expectations can you prune away to allow more room for your true priorities?

Take a 'snapshot' of your current life—include everything from your job tasks to social commitments. Now, identify the "weeds"—these are activities or expectations that aren't helping your "garden" thrive. Once you've spotted them, formulate a plan to gradually remove or reduce them. Document this plan in detail, specifying how and when you'll take action.

Section 4: The 90% Rule

The 90% Rule posits that we should be focusing only on activities and decisions that rate at least 90 on a scale of 100 in terms of their alignment with our personal or professional goals. This method forces us to be incredibly discerning, thereby saving our energy and time for what really matters. This section encourages you to evaluate your decisions and activities with a more critical eye.

1. Quantify Your Choices

As you evaluate an option, think about the single most important criterion for that decision, and then simply give the option a score between 0 and 100.

Try this method with a current life decision you're facing.

Confronted with a decision? Applying a numerical value can streamline the process and offer clarity. Identify the most pivotal criterion for your decision and score it between 0 and 100. What does this score reveal about its alignment with your life's priorities?

2. Clarifying Criteria

Criteria set the stage for our decisions. Establishing a clear set will guide you towards choices that truly resonate with your core values.

List three critical and three ideal criteria for a recent or upcoming decision.

Before making any major decision, it's crucial to establish a set of criteria that the choices should meet. Identify and list three essential criteria that are non-negotiable and three ideal criteria that would be a bonus. Reflect on how these criteria will steer your final decision.

3. The Impacts of Discernment

Discernment isn't just an action; it's a lifestyle. It demands constant engagement, but it returns the reward of a life more closely aligned with who you truly are.

How would your life change if you applied the 90% Rule to all your decisions?

Visualize your life under the governance of the 90% Rule, where you only pursue activities or make decisions that score at least a 90 in alignment with your values. Would this focus make your life simpler, more fulfilled, or even more challenging?

4. Negotiating Trade-Offs

Life is full of trade-offs. Recognizing them is the first step to making decisions that will align more closely with your true goals.

What are the 'trade-offs' you are currently experiencing, and how do they align with your essential goals?

Every choice you make comes with its own set of trade-offs. Identify these trade-offs in various facets of your life and consider how well or poorly they align with your ultimate goals.

5. The Burden and Liberation of Choice

We are condemned to be free. This freedom to choose is both a burden and a liberation, an ever-present duality that defines the human condition.

How does the power of choice manifest in your life?

Freedom offers us the potential to make choices that align with our authentic selves, but it can also feel overwhelming. Reflect on how this dual nature of freedom affects your life. Do you view your choices as opportunities or as burdens?

6. Living The Essentialist Philosophy

Essentialism is not just a strategy for success; it's a path for living a meaningful, fulfilling life.

Summarize in one paragraph how adopting essentialist principles can affect your life for the better.

Reflect on the cumulative effects of implementing the principles of Essentialism. Condense these reflections into a single paragraph that captures the transformative potential of living an essentialist life.

Conclusion

Congratulations on completing this course! May you continue to refine your focus, choose your vital few, and live a life that is less but truly better. Keep this journal as a compass to navigate through the complexities of life, always guiding you toward what is essential.
